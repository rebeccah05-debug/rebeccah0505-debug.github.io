Where Did the Design Go?

I love the art deco movement. I think it resulted in some of the best architecture and design from the last 100 years, however, it was a very short lived movement. Could you explain a little about why designers moved away from art deco and towards things like minimalist design, modern design, and anti-design? -A.P.

Overview

If you are a stranger to design movements, this newsletter starts with a general overview of key movements that shaped design throughout generations and insights into what design means for society.

What is Art Deco?

Art Deco was an optimistic design period that was characterized by opulence, glamour, and confidence- think empire state building. Designers, during this time, used rich materials and intense geometry meant to look luxurious. These design ideals came from an avant-garde convention in Paris. Art Deco was in its prime from 1920s until the start of World War II.

While a short timeline for a design movement, this is more indicative of the state of society, less that art deco was a failure.

NYC Empire State Building

The Optimism of Art Deco

Art Deco fascination came from the public's excitement in new ideas, industry, progress, and growing technologies. The materials and designs echoed these sentiments by focusing on aluminum, chrome, glass; materials previously never used for creations.

Designing with high-end materials signaled to the world we want progress. Buildings gleamed and reached toward the skies, similar to the dreams of the people that adored them.

Art Deco marked a turning point in design where designs were theatrical and emphasized adornment, instead of function.

But, such great optimism is bound to be shaken by tragedy.

Reality Changed Design Ideals

The scarcity of The Great Depression, followed by WWII, resulted in a force stop to this optimistic design movement. The emphasis on adornment became very hard to sell and justify when people were struggling to make ends meet. Resources were scarce leading to rationing that made any shows of opulence seem incredibly tone deaf.

A new solution became the forefront of design:

How can we design well for people, while using as little as possible?

This shift helps introduce the new movements that opposed Art Deco, that being minimalism and modernist design.

Modernism

Modernism emphasized structure, truth, and logic in design, the antithesis of Art Deco principles. Ornament seemed dishonest and a way to conceal design.

Barcelona Chair by Ludwig Mies Van Der Rohe

Designers and consumers turning to minimalism makes sense when you think about the consumer side of design. People and businesses were economically burdened. Minimal materials and straightforward forms were easy to replicate, thus easier and cheaper to mass produce.

Just like Art Deco, new beliefs opposed such minimal aesthetics leading to a shift in design principles. Seeing a trend yet?

Anti Design

Anti Design emerged as a reaction to the strict, minimal rules of Modernist design. Designers started to believe that focusing on structure negated the human aspect of design, like culture and emotion. Campana Brother's Stuffed Animal Chair

Similarly to Art Deco, Anti Design focused on the emotive, expressive aspects of design, but instead of centering around optimism, Anti design relies on satire and irony. Anyone remember when celebrities dressed "camp"? That was anti design!

Designers were sick of the adage "form follows function" because it often benefitted big money manufacturers that wanted more production. Anti-designers wanted people to really think about what they were seeing and buying, even if they thought the design was bad.

Art Deco Principles

Art Deco may have been fleeting, but designers continue to incorporate craft and visual complexity.

Principles of Art Deco with a lasting impression:

  • Design can be emotional
  • Design can evoke aspirations
  • Aesthetic joy has real cultural currency

Art Deco reminds us that when optimism returns, bold design usually follows.
